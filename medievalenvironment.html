Planning

1.1 Introduction

This project aims to create an interactive 3D co-op platform game using virtual reality (VR) technology. The unique gameplay mechanics involve one player using virtual reality (VR) to experience the environment without direct control over their movements, while a second player uses a game pad to navigate the player through the game world—an experience that requires communication and teamwork. The duo's shared objective is to successfully reach the exit door on the opposite side of the game environment (Figure 1.1.1), all while avoiding potential pitfalls along the way.
As part of the initial concept development, I have utilised the Workbench engine to render the game room layout. This includes views from the starting point to the exit, and a top-down perspective for a comprehensive understanding of the environment (refer to Figure 1.1.2).
As the 3D artist for this project, my primary responsibility lies in creating a game environment that authentically captures the rich aesthetics and thematic elements of the medieval era, while also enhancing the gameplay experience.

1.2 Theme

We have chosen the medieval period as the central theme for our project due to its unique artistic style and enduring recognition across cultures. This era is characterised by a rich tapestry of cultural references, providing a wealth of design elements that can enhance the gameplay experience.
My research draws on sources showcasing medieval room environments, highlighting both the rustic and ornate qualities that define this fascinating historical period. By carefully integrating these elements into our environment planning, I aim to transport players into a vibrant and authentic medieval world, thereby contributing to an engaging gameplay experience.

1.3 Research

As part of my responsibilities within the project, I have focused on creating a detailed game environment. This involved extensive research into medieval architectural styles and materials. My findings revealed that this period featured various architectural trends, including Romanesque, Gothic, and French styles, characterised by dramatic stone structures and rich wood elements. Key features of medieval buildings included spacious rooms made from timber or stone, often plastered, with exposed wooden ceiling beams, pointed or round arches, and small windows outfitted with wooden shutters.

To enhance the game’s atmosphere, I plan to incorporate vaulted wooden beams, creating a sense of grandeur and spaciousness in the virtual environment. Additionally, the later medieval period saw the introduction of brick, which can be used to add depth and character to our design. The combination of brick and wood will provide a visually striking and immersive experience for the players.

1.4 References

In the process of creating our medieval game environment, I gathered various reference materials that capture the essence of the Middle Ages. These references include details such as interior window shutters, diamond-patterned window grilles, and arched recesses commonly found in medieval architecture. Additionally, I focused on the use of wooden beams, which are characteristic of that era's structural designs. To further enhance the authenticity of our environment, I incorporated references depicting medieval rooms adorned with atmospheric lighting, brick walls, and stone flooring textures. These elements collectively contribute to a rich setting that reflects the historical context of the game.

1.5 Sketching

As part of my research, I produced a sketch outlining the design of our game room, emphasising the distinctive hammer beam construction. This initial illustration captures the architectural elements and layout envisioned for the space, laying a foundational blueprint for the game's environment. The sketch will guide the development process, ensuring that all elements harmonise with the medieval theme.

2.2 Arched Window Recess & Door Model

In the design of the medieval arched window recess and door, I utilised the same stone texture adopted for the flooring to ensure a cohesive aesthetic throughout the structure. By scaling down the UV-unwrapped sections of the arch meshes, I was able to create a seamless appearance, allowing the stone texture to resemble a singular, unified tile across each element.

To enhance the window frame and shutters, I selected a lighter wood texture that contributes to the overall charm of the architecture. Additionally, I developed bump and displacement maps using Photoshop, which provided depth and realism to the surfaces, enriching the visual complexity of the design.

For the details, such as the window diamond grille, door handle, and various decorative elements, I opted for a metal texture. This included a diffuse map for the primary metal colour, a metallic map to impart a realistic metallic sheen, and a roughness map to create a polished surface. To add intricate details like bumps and cracks, I applied a bump map, further enhancing the authenticity of the materials.

Regarding the hinges, I chose to use an image texture applied as a plane. This approach is particularly efficient in optimising geometry, especially for curved or rounded models. In the context of low-poly game design, as I had aimed for this project, this technique allowed for a reduction in polygon count while maintaining visual fidelity.

2.3 Candle Chandelier & Sconce

The finalised texturing of the candle chandelier (Figure 2.3.1) and wall sconce (Figure 2.3.2) showcases a meticulous application of materials and textures to enhance visual appeal. For both pieces, I employed an old metal texture, as depicted in Figure 2.3.3 (left). This texture is composed of four essential maps: diffuse, specular, normal, and displacement texture maps, which were correctly connected to their respective inputs in the shader setup to achieve a rich and authentic metallic finish.

The flame effect was achieved by creating a transparent plane using an image texture. The RGB colour information from the image was plugged into the base colour socket of the Principled BSDF shader, while the image's alpha channel was directed to the alpha input of the same shader, rendering the background transparent, as illustrated in Figure 2.3.3 (right). Additionally, to manipulate the intensity of the flame's glow, I connected the colour vector of the image texture to the emission input of the Principled BSDF. This approach allowed precise control over the luminosity of the flame, adding to the overall ambiance and realism of the chandelier and sconce design.

Scene Illumination

Effective lighting is paramount in achieving impactful final renders in 3D work. In this section, I explore various lighting techniques utilised to enhance the overall ambiance and realism of the scene.
A large rectangular area of light was positioned as the primary light source, casting a soft, diffused illumination that enveloped the entire environment. To focus on the chandelier models, square area lights were strategically placed, ensuring that these key features received the attention and detail they deserve.

For the candle chandeliers and sconces, I implemented point lights carefully positioned to simulate the warm glow of real candle flames, amplifying the scene's authenticity.

Additionally, I created a ray light using an emission node connected to a volume socket. This was further complemented by an area light to add depth and texture to the lighting scheme. To capture the essence of natural light for the exterior environment, I incorporated a sunlight with a sky texture, resulting in a balanced and cohesive lighting setup.

This systematic approach to lighting not only enhances the visual narrative of the scene, but also contributes to the quality and impact of the render.

3.1 Area Light Implementation

To establish primary illumination, I began by adding a rectangular area light on the ceiling. I increased its size along the x and y axes to effectively light the entire vaulted roof of the room. To ensure an even distribution of light, I rotated the area light by 35 degrees to accommodate the architectural features. During my initial render, I noticed that the texture of the ceiling lamp model was not adequately visible. To rectify this, I implemented square area lights for each chandelier using a three-point lighting technique, thereby enhancing the visibility and detail of these central features.

3.3 Point Light Creation Process

For the design of my chandeliers, which feature six candles each across three fixtures, I required 18 point lights. To streamline the process and enhance control over the lights during the design phase, I adopted the following method:


  • Creating the Base: I began by adding a plane mesh and pairing it with a point light, representing the candle flame.
  • Setting Up the Array: I then created an empty object, which would serve as the circular array offset for the lights. By applying an array modifier to the plane mesh, I specified the count as six to match the number of candles on each chandelier.
  • Positioning the Lights: In the object properties, I selected instancing and opted for 'faces' to position the point lights on each plane. Subsequently, I entered edit mode and adjusted the plane mesh’s position away from the world cursor.
  • Applying Modifiers: I returned to the array modifier, activated the object offset, and linked it to the empty object. This setup allowed the empty to control the plane mesh that housed the point lights. After creating the desired arrangement, I rotated and applied the array modifier to facilitate further modifications.
  • Final Adjustments: I added another array modifier with an increased count to finalise the setup.

This method simplifies the management of the lighting sources. All point lights can be controlled from the original point light, allowing for adjustments to power, colour, and radius with ease. When changes are made to the colour of the root source, for instance, all connected point lights will update accordingly, ensuring a cohesive lighting scheme throughout the space.

3.4 Ray Volume Light Creation

To introduce natural lighting into the environment, I opted to simulate sunlight streaming through windows. This involved using a sky texture complemented by sunlight to accurately represent daylight. I utilised a sun position add-on to carefully set the time of day, as well as the north offset and latitude for precise control. To create the visual effect of rays, I duplicated the window glass mesh, extruded it, and adjusted its position and rotation accordingly.

In shaded mode, I removed the previous texture and added an emission node, connecting it to the volume socket. I then refined the emission value to achieve the desired brightness. This technique not only enriched the lighting design, but also imbued the space with a sense of realism, as it effectively simulated the interplay of light and shadow typical of a sunlit atmosphere.

By integrating these diverse lighting techniques, I was able to evoke a complex narrative and mood, elevating the quality and impact of my 3D renders.

In response to team feedback, I made important adjustments to the game environment. The path generation script has been corrected to a 12x12 square format, replacing the previous rectangular design. For a polished presentation, I applied various compositing techniques using RGB curves, hue saturation adjustments, and colour balance nodes. These enhancements allow for a more vibrant and appealing colour scheme, improving the visual experience.

On the right side (Animation 5.1.2), I created a turntable animation for the candle chandelier, allowing viewers to appreciate its elegance from various angles. To achieve smooth camera movements for the turntable, I utilised a Bezier curve, ensuring a steady orbit around the chandelier. Following this, I linked the camera to the path using Ctrl + P to maintain a cohesive motion throughout the animation sequence.



Below, I describe and illustrate the methods used to create the flame motion and the flickering effect of the flame.

5.3 Flame Motion

To distort flame texture, I used noise texture. Firstly, I added a mix node and plugged it to the image texture vector socket.

I needed to coordinate the image texture as UV. So I added a texture coordinate node, and I plugged into the mix node to socket colour1.

Afterwards, I added a noise texture and plugged in to the mix node socket a colour2. Then I got an inappropriate distortion. I fixed that by reducing the mix node factor value and reduced scale amount.

Lastly, I added a driver into the noise texture scale bar.

Finally, my flame animated.

5.4 Flickering Effects

However, I wanted my flame to flick. So to do this, I added a key on emission strength.

Then I went to the graph editor and added a Built-In Function modifier.

So now my flame turns off and turns on. It is because shrink going until zero. It is not what I want.

Thus, I added a value offset. My texture strength now looks like flickering. Then I increased amplitude, which added flickering strength. Lastly, I restricted frame range by adding in and out range frames. I did it, because I wanted the flicker to appear slowly and fade out slowly.

I used a Bezier and circle curve for the camera movements. Then I set a camera to follow this path by pressing Ctrl P. Afterwards, I added empty and then set the camera focus element to this empty by adding a constraint Track To. By moving this empty, I controlled the camera focus to my models.
